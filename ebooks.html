A Guide to Naturally Cool Homes and Landscapes:

In the heart of South India, where the sun blazes through most of the year and monsoon winds offer fleeting relief, our homes should be a refuge cool, breathable, and in harmony with nature. Yet, the reality for most families today is far from this vision. We are increasingly living in cramped concrete boxes that trap heat, demand artificial cooling, and offer little to no connection with the outdoors. The irony is striking: centuries-old traditions like Vastu Shastra have always emphasized the movement of the sun and wind, the importance of shade-providing trees, and the strategic placement of rooms for natural ventilation. Unfortunately, rapid urbanization, high land prices, shrinking plot sizes, and poor planning have pushed these traditional principles to the sidelines. Today, most city layouts and plot developments are driven by profit margins, not climate logic. Builders focus on maximizing the saleable area, cutting plots into tight boxes 20x40, 30x40, 30x60 with little thought for green spaces or orientation. Government regulations, while strict on built-up limits and FAR (Floor Area Ratio), do little to encourage sustainable homes at an individual plot level. For plots under 8000 square feet, the open space reserve (OSR) requirement is converted into a fee. For larger plots, open spaces are handed over to municipalities, often resulting in poorly maintained parks far from individual homes. As a result, the average homeowner already battling limited income and rising construction costs rarely considers investing in natural cooling strategies. Most are forced to take bank loans, and under pressure to cut costs, compromise on quality, design, and sustainability. But what if we could shift this thinking? What if, instead of only counting square feet and concrete, we start thinking in terms of sun paths, wind channels, shaded courtyards, and edible gardens? What if you could build a home that stays 5 to 10 degrees cooler than your neighbor's house without depending on air conditioners or high electricity bills? What if your rooms always felt fresh, your plants thrived on harvested rainwater, and your health improved because of cleaner indoor air and lower humidity? This book is born out of a need to offer a practical, climate-responsive design guide for building naturally cool homes in South India. It combines traditional knowledge from Vastu with modern insights in climate science, architectural design, landscaping, and affordable construction. It challenges the belief that sustainable homes are expensive or only for the elite. In fact, nature-friendly homes can cost less in the long run, offering better comfort, fewer repairs, and lower running expenses. We believe a home is not just a building, it is a living, breathing system. The way we design our spaces directly impacts our health, energy use, and emotional well-being. Whether you’re building a 30x40 urban home or a 1-acre rural farmhouse, the principles of natural cooling can be applied to any scale. With the right orientation, zoning, material choices, plant selection, and room placements, anyone can create a cooler, greener, more affordable home. This book aims to walk you through that journey from understanding the climate and sun movement, to choosing the right plot, designing your building layout, selecting plants, and maintaining your garden all within the realistic constraints of today’s land and income limitations. It is a manual not just for architects and planners, but for homeowners, builders, and even policy-makers who care about building a better, cooler, and more sustainable future. Read More

Transforming India’s Sewerage & Drainage Infrastructure for a Healthier Future

For thousands of years, the growth of civilizations has been closely tied to the way they manage water from supplying clean drinking water to safely disposing of waste. From the advanced drainage systems of the Indus Valley Civilization to the underground sewers of ancient Rome, the story of urban progress is written beneath our feet. Yet, in modern India, despite rapid urban growth and economic progress, our sewerage and drainage systems lag dangerously behind demand. With swelling populations, poor urban planning, and outdated infrastructure, cities are choking under the weight of their own waste. Streets flood even with moderate rainfall, polluted waterbodies emit unbearable odours, and waterborne diseases continue to threaten public health. Poorly designed shallow drainage systems often no deeper than 2–3 meters struggle to keep up with the needs of modern cities. The solution may lie deeper quite literally. Across the world, cities like London, Singapore, and Dubai are implementing deep underground micro tunnelling systems, placed 20–30 meters below the surface, to carry massive volumes of wastewater safely away from urban cores. These systems not only prevent flooding and pollution but also reduce the need for disruptive surface works and save costs in the long term. This book traces the journey of sewerage and drainage evolution from the Stone Age to the engineering marvels of the present. We will explore how London transformed the filthy River Thames into a clean, thriving waterway; how ancient systems coped with waste; why India’s colonial-era drainage still outperforms many modern networks; and why deep-tunnel solutions are not yet widely adopted here. Drawing on global examples, cost-benefit analyses, and practical planning strategies, this work aims to present a clear roadmap for revolutionizing India’s underground infrastructure so that future generations inherit cities where water flows freely, streets remain dry, and rivers run clean.. Gardens and Soft Landscapes Design

n many parts of South India, home and building construction often focuses solely on bricks, beams, and budgets, while the outdoor spaces where life breathes are an afterthought. Landscaping is often mistaken for a luxury or a cosmetic add-on, something to be considered only if there's money left over after the “real” construction is complete. This is a costly misconception. Soft landscaping, which involves the living elements of a landscape such as soil, plants, lawns, trees, and flowers, is not just about aesthetics. It is about function, sustainability, climate comfort, biodiversity, and emotional wellbeing. A well-designed landscape improves air quality, controls temperatures, supports drainage and water conservation, and even increases property value. In a region like South India marked by heavy monsoons, hot summers, and fast urbanization landscaping is no longer optional; it is essential.
